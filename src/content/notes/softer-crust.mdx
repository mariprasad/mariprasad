I kept getting complaints that the crust was too hard. So I started watching for the things that cause it. These are my running notes.

## Steam early, or the crust sets too soon

Low-yeast and sourdough breads need **steam at the start of the bake**. Without it:

- the crust sets too early,
- moisture escapes from the surface,
- and you get a thick, leathery, knife-resistant crust.

So I steam for the **first 10–15 minutes**.

## Watch the bake time

Low-yeast dough **browns slower but dries faster** if it's baked too long. Slow colour isn't a reason to leave it in longer — it'll dry out before it looks "done".

## Small things that hold the moisture in

- **Dust with rice flour, not wheat flour** (and lightly) — wheat flour pulls moisture off the surface; rice flour doesn't.
- **Increase the hydration** when you're working with low yeast.
- **Don't lean only on strong flour** — it can make the crumb chewy. Mix your flours up.

## Don't let it cool in dry air

The crust hardens if the loaf **cools in dry air**. So:

- cool it on a rack but **drape a cloth loosely** over it, or
- **brush the crust** lightly with water or butter right after it comes out.

## My Dutch oven routine

- Preheat the Dutch oven to **220 °C**.
- Bake **25 min, lid ON**.
- Bake **10–15 min, lid OFF** — watch the colour.

It's aggressive heat, so **lid on for longer and less time open** builds a softer, less firm crust.
